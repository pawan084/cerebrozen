# Psychological safety

**Concept.** Psychological safety is the shared belief that a team is safe for
interpersonal risk-taking — that you can ask a question, admit a mistake, raise a
concern, or offer a half-formed idea without fear of being punished or humiliated.
It is the strongest single predictor of team learning and performance in the
research literature (Edmondson).

**Why it matters for a leader.** Safety is not niceness. Teams high in
psychological safety hold *higher* standards, because people surface problems early
instead of hiding them. When safety is low, the costs are invisible: the
unraised objection, the unreported error, the idea that never gets said.

**What builds it (coach toward these behaviours):**
- Frame work as a learning problem, not an execution problem ("we've never done
  this before, so we'll need everyone's read").
- Model fallibility out loud ("I may miss something — tell me when I do").
- Respond to bad news with curiosity, not blame — the first reaction to a raised
  risk trains everyone watching whether it is safe to raise the next one.
- Ask genuine questions and then actually change your mind sometimes.

**Coaching cue.** If a client describes a quiet team, a meeting where "no one
pushed back," or a surprise failure, the lever is usually safety, not skill.
